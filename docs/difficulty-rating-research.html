1 · The question

How should a brand-new question bank assign difficulty when there is no student-response data yet — for Indian competitive-exam MCQs (EAPCET/JEE/NEET style), on a platform that will quickly accumulate per-question correct/incorrect + time data once students practice? Specifically: is it defensible to show Easy/Medium/Hard labels before any data exists?

2 · What the evidence says

Definition

Difficulty is empirical by definition. Two standard measures: the classical facility / p-value (proportion who answer correctly) and the IRT b-parameter (ability level at which P(correct)=0.5). Both require observed responses.

Cappelleri et al., PMC4096146
Data cost

Rigorous IRT calibration needs a lot of data per item — ≈200 examinees for the simplest 1PL/Rasch model, ≈500 for 2PL, ≈1000+ for 3PL. Sample size (not test length) dominates accuracy. Simpler models are the right choice while data is thin.

Cappelleri et al. (PMC4096146); Hulin et al. 1982 (ERIC EJ1303861); Schroeders & Gnambs 2025
Experts are weak

Human experts predict difficulty poorly — and expertise makes it worse ("curse of knowledge"). Expert ratings explained only ~33% of empirical difficulty variance; the other 67% was unexplained. Fixed pre-data expert labels should not be treated as ground truth.

Wonde et al. 2024, BMC Med Educ (PMC11407004) — 200 MCQs, 7 expert physicians, 815 examinees
LLMs are weak (directly)

LLMs are poor at directly rating difficulty from text — the best system barely beat a "just predict the mean" baseline (RMSE 0.299 vs 0.311). A known failure mode: stronger models find items "too easy." (By contrast, response time IS predictable from text.)

BEA 2024 shared task, Yaneva et al. (ACL 2024.bea-1.39) — 667 retired USMLE items
Better LLM use (medium conf.)

LLM student-simulation (simulate learners across ability tiers, then fit IRT) predicts real difficulty far better than direct rating — item correctness correlations up to r≈0.75–0.82. Promising as a richer prior later, but heavier to build.

arXiv:2601.09953 (NAEP math, not yet peer-reviewed) — best-case ensemble figures
What real platforms do

Adaptive platforms solve cold-start with Elo/Glicko-style online ratings. Duolingo's "Birdbrain" is an IRT-inspired logistic model fit online by SGD — explicitly "a generalization of the Elo rating system": each question has a difficulty rating, each learner an ability rating; a wrong answer raises the item's difficulty and lowers the learner's ability. It converges in tens of attempts and corrects for who answered (raw % cannot).

IEEE Spectrum — Bicknell, Settles, Brust (Duolingo)
UI / labels

Do not surface fixed Easy/Medium/Hard before calibration. Keep difficulty as a continuous score in a "calibrating" state; reveal a discrete band only once the estimate is stable.

Synthesis of the above (expert ~33% variance; LLM ≈ baseline)

3 · Verdict

Difficulty can't be honestly labelled before data — not by experts (~33% of variance, worse with expertise) and not by LLMs (barely beat a mean baseline; they think everything's easy). The only trustworthy source is real student performance, captured through an Elo/Rasch rating that converges fast. Until a question is calibrated, show no difficulty label.

4 · Drut's approach (what we built)

StageWhat happens
Day 1 — cold startEach question stored difficultySource: 'uncalibrated', calibrated: false. No Easy/Medium/Hard shown. We keep the two things that are defensible: a model solve-time estimate → timeTargets (powers Sprint; time IS text-predictable) and concepts. The model's solve-confidence is used only as a quality gate to catch broken/unsolvable questions — never as a difficulty label.
As students practiceAn Elo / 1PL-Rasch engine (recompute-difficulty.mjs) rates each question against learner ability from real attempts (user_question_history). Fast convergence; corrects for who answered.
GraduationA question gets a shown Easy/Medium/Hard band only once calibrated (≥ ~20 attempts), set from the Elo-implied facility for a median learner. difficultySource flips to 'empirical-elo'.
Why this is the nearest-to-optimal: it's exactly what production adaptive systems do (Birdbrain = logistic/IRT via SGD = generalized Elo), it needs far less data than full IRT, it never asserts a difficulty we can't back, and it has one continuous metric that improves with every answer.

5 · In plain English

Nobody can guess how hard a question is before students try it — not subject experts (only ~33% accurate, and worse the more they know), not AI (it thinks everything is easy). So Drut ships questions with no difficulty label, keeps only the timer estimate (that the AI can predict), and lets a chess-style rating system score each question as real students answer — fast, self-correcting, and it only shows a difficulty badge once it has genuinely earned it.
