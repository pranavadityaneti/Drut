1 · The one-line takeaway

JEE Main is NOT "harder EAPCET." It's a different format (⅓ numerical-answer, with negative marking), a different syllabus basis (NCERT, trimmed in 2024 — vs EAPCET's state board), and a harder, more concept-driven paper. Our pipeline and concept backbone reuse cleanly; the questions and the practice format mostly do not. So "add JEE Main" = run JEE PYQs through an extended pipeline, not recycle EAPCET content.

3 · Syllabus: the deep difference [high]

EAPCET is built on the AP/Telangana state Intermediate curriculum (BIEAP/TSBIE), not NCERT. JEE Main is NCERT-based and NTA cut it in 2024. So the two chapter lists genuinely diverge:

  • EAPCET still tests units JEE Main dropped — confirmed concretely for Mathematical Induction (in EAPCET Maths; removed from JEE Main 2024). Also Mathematical Reasoning.
  • JEE Main 2024 cuts: Chemistry deepest (States of Matter, Surface Chemistry, Metallurgy, Hydrogen, s-Block, Environmental Chem, Polymers, Chemistry in Everyday Life — all gone); Physics lost Communication Systems + transistors/amplifiers; Maths lost Mathematical Induction + Mathematical Reasoning. Current: Maths 14 units, Physics 20, Chemistry 20.
Direct consequence for our code: our JEE_MAIN_TOPICS list is actually the EAPCET chapter list (it still contains Mathematical Induction). It is not a correct JEE Main chapter list. A real JEE Main bank needs a JEE-accurate, NCERT-2024 chapter taxonomy — not the EAPCET list reused verbatim. And pre-2024 JEE Main PYQs contain now-removed-syllabus questions → prefer 2024+ papers or filter.

4 · Difficulty [high]

Cross-platform consensus (competing portals, no incentive to agree): JEE Main is meaningfully harder. EAPCET questions are "more direct, formula-based, syllabus-bound"; JEE Main is "concept-driven, multi-step, in-depth." Implication: you can't serve EAPCET MCQs to a JEE aspirant and call it JEE prep — they're too easy and the format differs. (Our empirical Elo difficulty engine handles the per-exam difficulty gap automatically once attempts arrive — no special casing needed.)

5 · Chapter weightage

Physics [medium]: Mechanics ≈35%, Electromagnetism ≈27%, Modern Physics ≈17%. Most-asked chapters: Ray Optics & Optical Instruments (~19–23 Qs/yr aggregated), Units & Measurements (~13–20), Rotational Motion (~13–14).

Gap: Chemistry and Maths chapter-level weightage numbers failed verification and are deliberately omitted — treat as unresolved until sourced from official analyses. (We can also derive real weightage empirically once we have several JEE papers tagged in our own bank.)

6 · Market & competition [medium]

  • EAPCET: AP EAPCET 2025 ≈ 280k Engineering applicants (≈362k incl. Agri/Pharma). TS adds a similar order.
  • JEE Main: roughly an order of magnitude larger than each state exam (~10×).
  • Competitive bar: PW, Allen, Aakash, Embibe, Vedantu, Unacademy all compete hard on JEE Main PYQ banks + test series. A thin JEE offering won't impress serious aspirants — but a closed beta is about learning signal, not market share.

8 · Recommended sequencing

For the closed beta — JEE Main as an MCQ-only track:

  1. Wire jee_main as a supported exam (ExamDef + board mapping + exam selector).
  2. Build a JEE-accurate chapter taxonomy (NCERT 2024-reduced) — not the EAPCET list.
  3. Run 2024+ JEE Main PYQ MCQs (Section A) through the existing pipeline → stage → review. That's the 20-of-25-per-subject MCQ portion — a credible practice surface and widely how students drill.
  4. Defer NAT + negative-marking + percentile to post-beta; label the beta honestly as "JEE Main MCQ practice." NAT is a clean, well-scoped later add.
Why MCQ-first: it captures the national learning signal (10× pool) and reuses the whole pipeline at low marginal cost, without blocking beta on building a new question type + grading mode. The format gaps (NAT, negative marking) become the "earn the national claim" work before the July public launch.
In plain English: JEE Main and EAPCET test the same subjects but are genuinely different exams — JEE has fill-in-the-number questions, punishes wrong answers, follows NCERT (trimmed in 2024), and is harder. So we can't just recycle EAPCET questions into JEE. What we can reuse is the whole machine that turns past papers into practice. The smart beta move: add JEE Main as multiple-choice-only practice (two-thirds of the real paper) to learn from a much bigger audience, and build the numerical-answer part before we market "national" at the July launch.
