  2. B$4k+1$
  3. C$4k+2$
  4. D$4k+3$

  1. Two $n$th roots of unity lie on the unit circle, so a right angle at the origin means their arguments differ by $\frac{\pi}{2}$.
  2. Thus their ratio is $\pm i$, and that ratio must also be an $n$th root of unity.
  3. So $(\pm i)^n=1$, which happens exactly when $4\mid n$; hence $n=4k$.
🔬 Full Solution 2 steps
Use the fact that $n$th roots of unity are equally spaced on the unit circle, and a right angle at the origin means a quarter-turn between two such points.
Let $z_1=e^{2\pi i a/n}$ and $z_2=e^{2\pi i b/n}$ for integers $a,b$. Since the line segment joining them subtends a right angle at the origin, the angular difference between $z_1$ and $z_2$ is $\frac{\pi}{2}$, so their ratio must be $\pm i$.
$$\frac{z_1}{z_2}=e^{2\pi i(a-b)/n}=\pm i$$
Now $\pm i$ must itself be an $n$th root of unity. But $i$ is an $n$th root of unity exactly when $i^n=1$, which is equivalent to $4\mid n$. Hence $n=4k$ for some positive integer $k$.
$$i^n=1\iff 4\mid n$$
