Ex 1Physics · Kinematics + energyMedium
A ball is thrown vertically upward at $20\,\text{m/s}$ from the top of a $25\,\text{m}$ tower. With $g=10\,\text{m/s}^2$ and no air resistance, with what speed does it strike the ground?
  1. A$20\,\text{m/s}$
  2. B$30\,\text{m/s}$✓
  3. C$25\,\text{m/s}$
  4. D$35\,\text{m/s}$
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Only gravity acts, so the landing speed depends just on the start speed and the total drop — the trip up and back cancels.
  2. Use $v^2=u^2+2gh$ with $u=20$, $h=25$, $g=10$: $\;v^2=400+500=900$.
  3. $v=\sqrt{900}=30\,\text{m/s}$ → option B.
🔬 Full Solution
Only gravity does work, so mechanical energy is conserved — the final speed is fixed by the launch speed and the net drop, not the path. The clean tool is $v^2=u^2+2as$.
Only gravity acts, so the acceleration is constant at $g=10\,\text{m/s}^2$ pointing down; take downward as positive.
The upward leg doesn't change the result — the ball returns to its launch height with the same $20\,\text{m/s}$, so the motion is just a drop from the tower top at $u=20\,\text{m/s}$ through $s=25\,\text{m}$.
Substituting into the kinematic relation,
$$v^2=u^2+2as=20^2+2(10)(25)=400+500=900.$$
Taking the positive root, the ball strikes the ground at $v=\sqrt{900}=30\,\text{m/s}$.
Answer: $30\,\text{m/s}$ — option B.
Ex 2Physics · Laws of Motion + frictionHard
A $2\,\text{kg}$ block is released from rest at the top of a rough incline of length $8\,\text{m}$ inclined at $37^\circ$ to the horizontal ($\sin37^\circ=0.6,\ \cos37^\circ=0.8$). The coefficient of kinetic friction is $0.25$. Taking $g=10\,\text{m/s}^2$, what is its speed at the bottom?
  1. A$6\,\text{m/s}$
  2. B$8\,\text{m/s}$✓
  3. C$4\sqrt{5}\,\text{m/s}$
  4. D$10\,\text{m/s}$
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Net push along the slope = gravity component minus friction: $mg\sin\theta-\mu mg\cos\theta = 12-4 = 8\,\text{N}$.
  2. Acceleration $a=F/m=8/2=4\,\text{m/s}^2$; start from rest over $s=8\,\text{m}$.
  3. $v=\sqrt{2as}=\sqrt{2(4)(8)}=\sqrt{64}=8\,\text{m/s}$ → option B.
🔬 Full Solution
Two forces act along the incline: the component of gravity pulling the block down the slope, and kinetic friction resisting it. Find the net force, get the constant acceleration, then use $v^2=u^2+2as$. (Work–energy gives the same answer.)
Resolve the weight relative to the incline. With $\theta=37^\circ$, the part pulling the block down the slope is $mg\sin\theta$ and the part pressing it into the surface is $mg\cos\theta$:
$$mg\sin\theta=2(10)(0.6)=12\,\text{N},\qquad mg\cos\theta=2(10)(0.8)=16\,\text{N}.$$
The surface pushes back with a normal force equal to the perpendicular component, $N=mg\cos\theta=16\,\text{N}$, so kinetic friction — acting up the slope because the block slides down — is
$$f=\mu N=0.25\times16=4\,\text{N}.$$
The driving component ($12\,\text{N}$) beats the maximum friction, so the block does move. The net force along the incline is
$$F_{\text{net}}=mg\sin\theta-f=12-4=8\,\text{N}.$$
Newton's second law then fixes a constant acceleration down the slope:
$$a=\frac{F_{\text{net}}}{m}=\frac{8}{2}=4\,\text{m/s}^2.$$
The block starts from rest ($u=0$) and slides the full length $s=8\,\text{m}$, so
$$v^2=u^2+2as=0+2(4)(8)=64\,\text{m}^2/\text{s}^2.$$
Taking the positive root gives the speed at the bottom, $v=\sqrt{64}=8\,\text{m/s}$.
Answer: $8\,\text{m/s}$ — option B.
