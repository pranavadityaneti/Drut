Q1 Physics · Laws of Motion · Newton's second law Easy
A trolley of mass $4\,\text{kg}$ on a frictionless track is pulled by a constant net force of $12\,\text{N}$. What is its acceleration?
  1. A $2\,\text{m/s}^2$
  2. B $3\,\text{m/s}^2$✓ correct
  3. C $6\,\text{m/s}^2$
  4. D $12\,\text{m/s}^2$
Concept Newton's second law links the net external force to acceleration through $F_{\text{net}}=ma$. For a known force and mass, the quickest move is to divide force by mass to get $a$.

⚡ Quick Method (T.A.R.)

  1. Trigger: A net force and mass are given, so use Newton's second law $F_{\text{net}}=ma$.
  2. Action: Rearrange to $a=F_{\text{net}}/m$ and substitute $12\,\text{N}$ and $4\,\text{kg}$.
  3. Result: $a=12/4=3\,\text{m/s}^2$.

🔬 Full Solution (D.E.E.P.)

DIAGNOSE
Use Newton's second law, $F_{\text{net}}=ma$, because the problem gives the net force and the mass and asks for acceleration.
EXTRACT
Given $F_{\text{net}}=12\,\text{N}$ and $m=4\,\text{kg}$; unknown is $a$.
EXECUTE
Rearrange to $a=\frac{F_{\text{net}}}{m}=\frac{12}{4}=3\,\text{m/s}^2$.
PROOF
Check by substitution: $ma=4\times3=12\,\text{N}$, which matches the given net force.
Q2 Physics · Motion in a Plane · Projectile motion Medium

  4. D $80\,\text{m}$
Concept Use the trajectory form $y = x\tan\theta - \frac{g x^2}{2u^2\cos^2\theta}$ to convert a height condition into a quadratic in $x$. The two roots give the entry and exit points at that height.

  2. Action: Substitute $u$, $\theta$, $g$, and the height into $y = x\tan\theta - \frac{g x^2}{2u^2\cos^2\theta}$, then solve the quadratic.
  3. Result: Take the larger root to get the second intersection point.

🔬 Full Solution (D.E.E.P.)

DIAGNOSE
The governing principle is projectile trajectory, which is a parabola: $y = x\tan\theta - \frac{g x^2}{2u^2\cos^2\theta}$. Since a fixed height can be hit twice, we must find the two $x$-values and choose the later one.
EXTRACT

Check in $y = x - \frac{x^2}{80}$: at $x = 20$, $y = 20 - 5 = 15$; at $x = 60$, $y = 60 - 45 = 15$. The later crossing is therefore $60\,\text{m}$, matching option 3.
Q3 Physics · Work, Energy and Power · Work-energy theorem Hard
A $2\,\text{kg}$ cart moves on a frictionless straight track. The horizontal force on it is $F(x)=8-2x\,\text{N}$, where $x$ is the displacement in metres from the start. If its speed at $x=0$ is $1\,\text{m/s}$, at what displacement does it first come to rest?
  1. A $4+\sqrt{17}\,\text{m}$✓ correct
  2. B $4+\sqrt{15}\,\text{m}$
  3. C $8\,\text{m}$
  4. D $4+\sqrt{18}\,\text{m}$
Concept For a force that changes with displacement, the net work is the area under the $F$-$x$ curve, or $W=\int F\,dx$. The stopping point follows from $K_i+W=0$, not from $F=0$.

⚡ Quick Method (T.A.R.)

  1. Trigger: Force is given as a function of displacement and the cart stops, so use $W_{\text{net}}=\Delta K$.
  2. Action: Compute $\int_0^x (8-2u)\,du$ and set it equal to $-\tfrac12(2)(1^2)$.
  3. Result: Solve $x^2-8x-1=0$; the physical root is $x=4+\sqrt{17}\,\text{m}$.

🔬 Full Solution (D.E.E.P.)

DIAGNOSE
Use the work-energy theorem for a variable force: $W_{\text{net}}=\Delta K$ and $W=\int F\,dx$. Since the cart first stops, final kinetic energy is $0$.
EXTRACT
Initial kinetic energy is $K_i=\tfrac12 mv^2=\tfrac12(2)(1^2)=1\,\text{J}$. For displacement $x$, the work done by the force is $W(x)=\int_0^x (8-2u)\,du$.
EXECUTE
Evaluate $W(x)=8x-x^2$. At the stopping point, $K_f-K_i=0-1=W(x)$, so $8x-x^2=-1$. Rearranging gives $x^2-8x-1=0$, hence $x=4\pm\sqrt{17}$.
PROOF
The negative root is not physical because displacement from the start cannot be negative here. Therefore the first stopping point is $x=4+\sqrt{17}\,\text{m}$.

  2. Action: Replace each quantity by its dimensions and solve $[\alpha]=[E]/([p]^2[V])$.
  3. Result: After simplification, $[\alpha]=[M^{-1} L T^2]$.

🔬 Full Solution (D.E.E.P.)

DIAGNOSE

🔬 Full Solution (D.E.E.P.)

DIAGNOSE

EXTRACT

EXECUTE
Hence $K/E = \frac{9}{16}$ and $U/E = \frac{7}{16}$. Therefore, $U:K = 7:9$.
PROOF

Q7 Physics · Thermodynamics · First law Hard
A sample of gas changes from state $A(V_0,P_0)$ to state $B(3V_0,3P_0)$. Along the direct straight-line path $A \to B$ in the $P$-$V$ plane, $8P_0V_0$ of heat is supplied. If the same change of state is carried out instead by first expanding at constant pressure $P_0$ from $V_0$ to $3V_0$ and then raising the pressure at constant volume $3V_0$ to $3P_0$, the heat absorbed is:
  1. A $6P_0V_0$✓ correct
  2. B $4P_0V_0$
  3. C $8P_0V_0$
  4. D $2P_0V_0$
Concept Use $ΔU = Q - W$ with $W = \int P\,dV$. For fixed initial and final states, $ΔU$ stays the same even when the path changes, but $Q$ changes because the work changes.

⚡ Quick Method (T.A.R.)

  1. Trigger: Same initial and final states are given, but the path changes, so first find $\Delta U$ from the known path.
  2. Action: Compute work from each $P$-$V$ path: for the straight line use the trapezium area, and for the two-step path use only the horizontal segment; then apply $\Delta U = Q - W$.
  3. Result: Get $\Delta U=4P_0V_0$ from the direct path, then add the alternate-path work $2P_0V_0$ to get $Q=6P_0V_0$.

🔬 Full Solution (D.E.E.P.)

DIAGNOSE
First law of thermodynamics governs the problem: $\Delta U = Q - W$, where $W$ is the work done by the gas. For a $P$-$V$ process, $W$ equals the area under the path.
EXTRACT
For the direct straight-line path from $(V_0,P_0)$ to $(3V_0,3P_0)$, the work is the area of a trapezium: $W_1 = \frac{P_0+3P_0}{2}\,(3V_0-V_0)=4P_0V_0$. The heat supplied there is $Q_1=8P_0V_0$.
EXECUTE
So the state change has $\Delta U = Q_1 - W_1 = 8P_0V_0 - 4P_0V_0 = 4P_0V_0$. For the alternate path, the isobaric expansion does work $W_2=P_0(3V_0-V_0)=2P_0V_0$, while the isochoric step does no work. Hence $Q_2 = \Delta U + W_2 = 4P_0V_0 + 2P_0V_0 = 6P_0V_0$.
PROOF
Check consistency: using the alternate path, $Q_2 - W_2 = 6P_0V_0 - 2P_0V_0 = 4P_0V_0$, the same $\Delta U$ found from the direct path. Therefore the answer is $6P_0V_0$.
