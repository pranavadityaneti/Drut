Major revision to the original report

The original report said: "Geography is our moat — Educato doesn't touch EAPCET / JEE / NEET / India."

The reality: Educato's app is at educato.com/app/iat/iat-exam/journey. "IAT" is an Indian-syllabus aptitude/admission test. The Math I topic list — Sets, Relations, Functions, Angle Measures (degrees/radians), Real Functions, Algebra of Real Functions — is verbatim Indian 11th-grade curriculum. The current question I attempted is tagged #2017-eng-iat — a real past paper. Educato is already serving Indian-syllabus content.

Our moat is therefore narrower than I claimed: not "India vs not-India," but "EAPCET + JEE + NEET + KCET + CUET — deeply localised — at ₹299/mo." That's still defensible; price + depth-of-EAPCET coverage remain ours. But the "they're not even in India" framing was wrong, and the urgency to ship is higher than my original report implied.

1. Lesson architecture — the pedagogical secret in one screen

A 4-9 minute "lesson" in Educato is not a static essay. It's a 6-step Study Session that interleaves Concept blocks with Question blocks. The active-recall + retrieval-practice pedagogy is baked into the very structure of the lesson.

Concrete example — the "Introduction to Sets and Their Representations" lesson I opened has these 6 steps in order:

StepTypeTitleNotes
1ConceptIntroduction to SetsItself a 5-page mini-tutorial (right-rail pagination dots) with: hook quote → definition → history → illustration → ...
2QuestionA function f satisfying f∘f∘f(x) = x for all x ∈ ℝ isReal past-paper MCQ, tagged with exam-source
3ConceptDefining Sets and Basic Notation—
4ConceptRepresenting Sets—
5ConceptExamples of Set Representations—
6—(next session boundary)—
This is the most replicable insight in the entire audit. Drut currently has "Practice" (questions only) and "Sprint" (timed questions only). We have no "Lesson" surface. Adding a Lesson surface that interleaves 3-5 Concept blocks with 1-2 retrieval-practice Questions would be a major upgrade — and the questions for it already exist in our bank. The Concept text is what we'd need to generate.

3. Inside a Question card

The question I attempted (real past paper from 2017 IAT English exam):

A function $f$ satisfying $f∘f∘f(x) = x$ for all $x ∈ ℝ$ is

A onto but not one–one.   B neither one–one nor onto.   C one–one and onto.   D one–one but not onto.

Tagged: #Anciens examens · #2017-eng-iat

Things to note about the question UX

  • Provenance tags are public. Students see #2017-eng-iat and know they're practicing a real exam question. Strong trust signal. Drut should tag our PYQ-sourced questions visibly ("AP EAPCET 2024" etc.) — and tag AI-generated ones with "AI-generated, expert reviewed" or similar so it's transparent.
  • The tag language is French — "Anciens examens" = "Old exams". Confirms the founders' internal taxonomy comes from their French/Italian background, even on English content.
  • "Add to note" button top-right — they have a personal-notes feature where students can save questions for later review. Drut should add this.
  • Energy cost on the Submit button — ⚡2. Same as sending an AI chat message. Every action has an energy price.
  • No timer, no hint button in this Learn mode. Clean single-CTA UX.
  • 👍/👎 chips below the options — same "rate this question" pattern as concept blocks. They train their pipeline on every interaction.

4. The wrong-answer screen — and the killer differentiator nobody talks about

I deliberately picked B (the wrong answer). What loaded was the most informative single screen in the entire app.

Structure of the wrong-answer feedback

  1. Top status: "❌ Not quite" (red) with this-session score "0/4"
  2. "You missed:" block — shows the correct letter + the canonical explanation in 1 sentence:
    C — Correct. If f∘f∘f(x) = x for all x, then f is bijective because every element has a unique preimage and image under repeated application of f.
  3. "You selected incorrectly:" block — names your wrong letter + a 1-sentence specific refutation:
    B — Incorrect. The function is both one–one and onto as shown by the property f∘f∘f(x) = x for all x.
  4. Full per-option breakdown — every option re-listed with its own short explanation. Crucially:

The killer differentiator: per-option crowd statistics

OptionText% of students who pickedState
Aonto but not one–one24%wrong (no inline note)
B (my pick)neither one–one nor onto14%❌ red ring + inline "Incorrect" note
C ✓one–one and onto57%✅ green check + full explanation
Done–one but not onto5%wrong (no inline note)
This is the feature I most underestimated. Showing "14% of students made this exact mistake" alongside the explanation creates a social-proof + diagnostic moment in one. You don't feel alone in your error; you feel like you fell into a predicted trap — which is far more memorable than a generic "wrong answer" screen.

Cost to build in Drut: small. A new aggregate column option_a_picks / option_b_picks / option_c_picks / option_d_picks on cached_questions (or a separate question_option_stats table) updated on every answer. Compute % at render time. ~2–3 days of work. I'm upgrading this to Priority 1.5 in the replication plan.

5. "Plumi" — the AI tutor in detail

The mascot bottom-right opens a side-panel chat. Branded as Plumi. Highlights:

Named persona

"Hi! I'm Plumi, your AI tutor." Named + mascot. Drut should name ours.

Context pill

"📋 Viewing question — [question text]" — explicitly shown above the chat so it's obvious what context the AI has.

Prompt-starter pills

5 canned starters: "What's the best way to study?", "Quiz me on…", "Help me connect…", "Create a comparison…", "Explain…"

Voice input

Mic icon. Speak your question — saves typing math notation.

"Internet" toggle

Opt-in live web search. Power-user feature, off by default.

Energy cost

Every message costs ⚡2, same as submitting an answer. Premium = unlimited.

What Plumi actually said when I asked "Why is C correct and not B? Walk me through it step by step."

Plumi: Let me break this down carefully. The key insight: If f∘f∘f(x) = x for all x ∈ ℝ, then applying f three times gets you back to where you started.

Why the function must be one-to-one (injective): Suppose f(a) = f(b). Apply f twice more to both sides:

  • f(f(f(a))) = f(f(f(b)))
  • But we know f(f(f(a))) = a and f(f(f(b))) = b
  • So a = b

This proves f is one-to-one. ✓

Why the function must be onto (surjective): For any y ∈ ℝ, we need to find some x such that f(x) = y. Here's the trick: let x = f(f(y)). Then:

  • f(x) = f(f(f(y))) = y

So we found an x (namely, f(f(y))) that maps to y. This proves f is onto. ✓

Conclusion: Since f is both one-to-one AND onto, it's bijective — so the answer is C. Does this make sense? The property f³ = identity forces both directions to work.

Generated in ~10 seconds. ~150 words. LaTeX rendered correctly.

Quality assessment of Plumi's response

  1. Names the key insight first — pedagogically perfect framing before the proof.
  2. Splits into two named proof halves with bold headers and ✓ checkmarks per sub-goal.
  3. Uses real proof technique — "assume f(a)=f(b), derive a=b" is exactly how a mathematician proves injectivity. Not hand-wavy.
  4. "Here's the trick" framing for the surjectivity proof — explicitly flags where the cleverness lives. Most students wouldn't think to set x = f(f(y)).
  5. Conceptual takeaway at the end — "f³ = identity forces both directions to work" — gives the moral, not just the steps.
  6. Conversational sign-off ("Does this make sense?") invites follow-up. Treats this as a dialogue.
  7. Properly typeset LaTeX rendering throughout. They have KaTeX or MathJax wired.

Model inference: The response quality (structure + tone + "Here's the trick" framing) + clean LaTeX is consistent with GPT-4o or Claude 3.5 Sonnet+ with a thoughtful system prompt that biases toward structured proofs with checkmarks. Not a small/cheap model. The prompt almost certainly includes the question, the canonical explanation, the user's chosen answer, and instructions like "name the key insight first, structure proofs clearly, end with a conceptual takeaway, invite follow-up."

6. Gamification — the energy economy

Every action shows an energy badge ⚡N:

  • Submitting an answer: ⚡2
  • Sending a chat message to Plumi: ⚡2
  • (Other actions probably costed too — I didn't poke further)

This is the Duolingo "hearts" mechanic. It creates a daily-quota ceiling for free users, which is the entire upsell for Premium (presumably: unlimited energy). The badge is small and elegant — doesn't dominate the UI — but always visible.

Drut's 15/day quota already does this conceptually but invisibly. We should consider surfacing a small energy meter on the user's avatar (e.g. "⚡11/15") so the constraint is felt, not just discovered when the paywall fires. Makes the upgrade choice clearer.

9. The post-question concepts (steps 3, 4, 5) — what I missed the first time

Pranav called this out: in the first pass I jumped from Concept 1 to the Question and called it done. The post-question concepts and the session-completion screen contain the most important findings of the whole audit. Captured in full on a second pass.

Step 3 — Concept: "Defining Sets and Basic Notation"

Style: opens with everyday-life examples ("a pack of cards, a crowd of people, or a cricket team") that are deliberately localised to India — explicitly references "the rivers of India" (Ganga vs Nile). Then enumerates six well-defined collections (including a quadratic equation's solution), defines a set, and presents a sharp counter-example ("the five most renowned mathematicians of the world" — not well-defined, therefore not a set). Closes with notation conventions (∈ vs ∉, capital vs lowercase) and a reference table of standard symbols (N, Z, Q, R, Z⁺, Q⁺, R⁺).

Pedagogical moveImplementation
Hook → Definition → Counter-example → Notation → Reference tableSame 5-beat structure as Concept 1 (Hardy quote → definition → history → image). Their concept card schema is consistent.
Inline highlighted callout"Concept: A set is a well-defined collection of objects." — visually emphasised as the takeaway sentence.
Counter-example pedagogyShows what's NOT a set and explains why ("most renowned" is subjective). More memorable than just stating the rule.
Immediate notation worked exampleV (vowels) and P (prime factors of 30) used to demo ∈/∉ within sentences of being introduced.

Step 4 — Concept: "Representing Sets"

Style: introduces the two representation systems (Roster form, Set-builder form), each with definition → worked examples → "Note" callouts for common pitfalls. Demonstrates the same three sets (vowels, divisors of 42, odd naturals) in both notations — pedagogically excellent: same content, two representations, reinforcing the concept.

Key didactic detail: Concept 4 explicitly references the (a), (b), (c) numbering from Concept 3. The two concepts are tightly chained. The "SCHOOL → {S, C, H, O, L}" example for the no-repetition rule is a memorable mnemonic.

Step 5 — Concept: "Examples of Set Representations"

Style: five worked examples (quadratic solution set; set defined by an inequality; squares of naturals; n/(n+1) fractions; a 4-pair matching exercise). Pure application — no new theory.

The most important micro-discovery in Concept 5: Example 5 is itself a mini-quiz embedded inside a "Concept" card — a 4-pair matching exercise (Roster ↔ Set-builder). Active recall isn't just at the lesson level; it's slipped into the concept content itself. The interleaving runs two levels deep.

The definitive answer to "is the lesson adaptive?"

Was I right to assume…?Reality

10. Step 6 — "Session Complete!" — the master narrative reveals itself

The completion screen rewrites the audit's biggest takeaway. The whole product's emotional payoff loop runs through one specific UI element: your predicted grade moves in real time, attributed to a specific topic.

ElementWhat I saw
Headline message"Your grade ↘ went down by 9 points, driven by your answers in Sets and Logic." — with a literal red down-arrow.
Three stat cardsGRADE: 111 (red ↓ triangle — was 120 before this session) · QUESTIONS: 5 ✓ · TIME: 1 min
MascotPlumi centred, eyes closed in a peaceful pose — not disappointed, even though I failed. Brand voice stays encouraging regardless of outcome.
Primary CTA"Continue to Next Step" — the loop hook. No "Take a break" / "Review what you learned" / "Reflect" CTA. The product wants you to immediately start the next lesson.
The pedagogical model in one sentence:

Educato runs a static curriculum on top of a hyper-responsive predictive grade engine — and the grade is what makes every action feel consequential.

You don't "level up" or "earn XP" — your predicted EAPCET-equivalent score moves. Every question is a tiny bet on your exam result. Get one wrong: –9 points, attributed to Sets and Logic. Get one right: +X points. Every session ends with this verdict.

This is a stronger retention loop than streaks or hearts. Drut should build this.

What it implies about their backend

  • Topic-weighted predictive model — they have a model that maps individual question correctness/timing to a predicted exam score, with per-topic weights ("driven by your answers in Sets and Logic").
  • Real-time update — the grade number changes within seconds of submitting an answer.
  • Visible target — earlier dashboard showed "Grade estimate 120 / Target 227" so the user always knows the delta to goal.

How buildable is this for Drut?

Surprisingly buildable for a credible MVP. A first-pass implementation:

  1. Compute a baseline expected score = (chapter weight in EAPCET × current mastery per chapter) summed across all chapters. Mastery per chapter = correct_attempts / total_attempts × difficulty_weighting.
  2. Recompute on every answer submission.
  3. Show the delta on the session-complete screen with topic attribution.

Build estimate: ~5 days for v1 (without ML). A more sophisticated version with IRT (Item Response Theory) takes ~3 weeks. The v1 is good enough for beta because students aren't comparing the prediction's accuracy to past EAPCET data — they're reacting to the movement.

Closing observation

The deepest takeaway from the full walkthrough is: Educato's content is mostly static; their drama is fully dynamic. The Concept cards are a well-written textbook chunked into 4–9 minute servings. The Question cards are normal MCQ. The AI tutor is a good system prompt over a strong LLM. None of these would be groundbreaking on their own. But everything resolves to a single number — your predicted exam grade — that moves in real time after every answer, with topic attribution. That's the master narrative. That's the engineering investment we most need to match. The micro-pedagogy is replicable in weeks. The predictive grade with its real-time topic attribution is the moat we should out-build them on, because the EAPCET data (past cut-offs, topic-wise mark distributions, question difficulty by year) is far better understood in our market than the global "10,000 exams" they're spread across.
