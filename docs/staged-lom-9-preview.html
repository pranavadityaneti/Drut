Q1Easy 🔁 verifier: agrees B · high conf
A book is kept at rest on a horizontal table. Which pair of forces forms a Newton's third-law pair?
  1. A The weight of the book and the normal force on the book
  2. B The force exerted by the book on the table and the force exerted by the table on the book ✓
  3. C The weight of the book and the gravitational pull of Earth on the book
  4. D The normal force on the book and the frictional force on the book
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Check whether the two forces act on the same body or on two different bodies.
  2. Recall that a Newton's third-law pair always comes from one interaction and acts on different bodies.
  3. Select the option with the book pushing the table and the table pushing the book.
🔬 Full Solution
Newton's third-law pairs are equal and opposite forces arising from the same interaction, but they act on different bodies. So the fastest route is to identify which option separates the two bodies involved.
A third-law pair cannot both act on the same object. If two forces are drawn on the book alone, they may balance each other, but they are not an action-reaction pair.
For the book-table contact, the book exerts a downward force on the table, and the table exerts an upward force on the book. These two forces are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.
$$F_{\text{book on table}}=-F_{\text{table on book}}$$
The weight of the book and the normal force on the book both act on the book, so they are not a third-law pair. The gravitational pull of Earth on the book is simply the weight of the book, not a separate paired force.
Therefore, the correct Newton's third-law pair is the force of the book on the table and the force of the table on the book.
Option B is correct: the force exerted by the book on the table and the force exerted by the table on the book.
Distractor rationale
  1. A Incorrect. Weight and normal force act on the same body, the book. They may cancel in equilibrium, but they are not an action-reaction pair.
  2. B Correct. These two forces arise from the same contact interaction and act on different bodies: the book and the table.
  3. C Incorrect. The weight of the book is the gravitational pull of Earth on the book, so these are not two different forces forming a pair.
  4. D Incorrect. Normal force and friction are different forces on the book; they are not equal-and-opposite forces of one interaction pair.
Q2Easy 🔁 verifier: agrees B · high conf
A $3\,\text{kg}$ cart on a smooth track speeds up uniformly from $2\,\text{m s}^{-1}$ to $6\,\text{m s}^{-1}$ in $2\,\text{s}$. What is the net force acting on it?
  1. A $3\,\text{N}$
  2. B $6\,\text{N}$ ✓
  3. C $9\,\text{N}$
  4. D $12\,\text{N}$
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Find the velocity change: $6 - 2 = 4\,\text{m s}^{-1}$.
  2. Divide by $2\,\text{s}$ to get $a = 2\,\text{m s}^{-2}$.
  3. Use $F = ma = 3 \times 2 = 6\,\text{N}$.
🔬 Full Solution
Use Newton's second law. Since the cart speeds up uniformly, first find its acceleration from the change in velocity and then multiply by the mass.
The cart's speed increases from $2\,\text{m s}^{-1}$ to $6\,\text{m s}^{-1}$, so the change in velocity is $4\,\text{m s}^{-1}$.
$$\Delta v = 6 - 2 = 4\,\text{m s}^{-1}$$
Because this change happens uniformly in $2\,\text{s}$, the acceleration is the change in velocity per unit time.
$$a = \frac{\Delta v}{t} = \frac{4}{2} = 2\,\text{m s}^{-2}$$
Now apply $F = ma$ for the net force on the cart.
$$F = ma = 3 \times 2 = 6\,\text{N}$$
The net force is $6\,\text{N}$, so the correct option is B.
Distractor rationale
  1. A Option A comes from using the initial speed directly: $F = \frac{mv_i}{t} = \frac{3 \times 2}{2} = 3\,\text{N}$.
  2. B Option B is correct: $\Delta v = 6 - 2 = 4\,\text{m s}^{-1}$, so $a = 2\,\text{m s}^{-2}$ and $F = 3 \times 2 = 6\,\text{N}$.
  3. C Option C comes from using the final speed directly: $F = \frac{mv_f}{t} = \frac{3 \times 6}{2} = 9\,\text{N}$.
  4. D Option D comes from forgetting to divide by time and taking $F = m\Delta v = 3 \times 4 = 12\,\text{N}$.
Q3Easy 🔁 verifier: agrees A · high conf
A $4\,\text{kg}$ trolley on a smooth horizontal track experiences a constant net force of $10\,\text{N}$. What is its acceleration?
  1. A $2.5\,\text{m s}^{-2}$ ✓
  2. B $40\,\text{m s}^{-2}$
  3. C $10\,\text{m s}^{-2}$
  4. D $0.4\,\text{m s}^{-2}$
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Use Newton's second law, $F=ma$.
  2. Rearrange to $a=\frac{F}{m}$ and substitute the values.
  3. Compute $a=\frac{10}{4}=2.5\,\text{m s}^{-2}$.
🔬 Full Solution
This is a direct application of Newton's second law. Since the track is smooth, the given force is the net force, so acceleration follows from $F=ma$.
On a smooth track, there is no friction to alter the horizontal force balance, so the net force is $10\,\text{N}$.
$$F = ma$$
Rearrange the relation to solve for acceleration and substitute the given mass and force.
$$a = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{10}{4}$$
Evaluating the fraction gives the acceleration as $2.5\,\text{m s}^{-2}$.
$$a = 2.5\,\text{m s}^{-2}$$
The acceleration is $2.5\,\text{m s}^{-2}$, so option A is correct.
Distractor rationale
  1. A Correct: using $a=\frac{F}{m}$ gives $a=\frac{10}{4}=2.5\,\text{m s}^{-2}$.
  2. B Wrong: this comes from multiplying the numbers, $10\times 4=40$, instead of dividing.
  3. C Wrong: this treats the acceleration as the force value itself, giving $10$.
  4. D Wrong: this inverts the fraction and uses $\frac{4}{10}=0.4$.
Q4Medium 🔁 verifier: agrees A · high conf
A $4\,\text{kg}$ block $A$ on a rough horizontal table is tied by a light inextensible string over a smooth pulley to a hanging $2\,\text{kg}$ block $B$. If the coefficient of kinetic friction between $A$ and the table is $0.25$ and $g = 10\,\text{m/s}^2$, what is the tension in the string just after release?
  1. A $\frac{50}{3}\,\text{N}$ ✓
  2. B $\frac{40}{3}\,\text{N}$
  3. C $20\,\text{N}$
  4. D $30\,\text{N}$
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Find friction on the $4\,\text{kg}$ block using $f=\mu m g$.
  2. Use the whole system to get $a=(m_B g-f)/(m_A+m_B)$.
  3. Use $m_B g-T=m_B a$ and solve for $T$.
🔬 Full Solution
The two blocks share the same acceleration, so Newton's second law is the cleanest route. First compute the frictional force on the table block, then use the two-block system to get $a$ and finally find the tension from the hanging block.
Block $A$ moves on a rough surface, so friction acts opposite to the motion. Its magnitude is found from $f=\mu m_A g$.
$$f = 0.25 \times 4 \times 10 = 10\,\text{N}$$
For the two blocks taken together, tension is internal and cancels out. The only external force driving the system is the hanging weight minus the friction on $A$.
$$a = \frac{m_B g - f}{m_A + m_B} = \frac{20 - 10}{4 + 2} = \frac{5}{3}\,\text{m/s}^2$$
Now isolate the hanging $2\,\text{kg}$ block. Since it moves downward, its weight is larger than the tension by exactly $m_B a$.
$$m_B g - T = m_B a$$
Substitute the acceleration and solve for the string tension. This gives the required value directly.
$$T = 20 - 2\left(\frac{5}{3}\right) = \frac{50}{3}\,\text{N}$$
The tension is $\frac{50}{3}\,\text{N}$, so the correct choice is option A.
Distractor rationale
  1. A Correct: using $f=\mu m_A g=10\,\text{N}$ gives $a=\frac{20-10}{6}=\frac{5}{3}\,\text{m/s}^2$, and then $T=20-2a=\frac{50}{3}\,\text{N}$.
  2. B This is what you get if friction is ignored: $a=\frac{20}{6}=\frac{10}{3}\,\text{m/s}^2$, so $T=20-2a=\frac{40}{3}\,\text{N}$.
  3. C This comes from the false equilibrium idea for the hanging block, giving $T=m_B g=2\times 10=20\,\text{N}$.
  4. D This results from wrongly adding friction to the pull: $a=\frac{20+10}{6}=5\,\text{m/s}^2$, so $T=4a+10=30\,\text{N}$.
Q5Medium 🔁 verifier: agrees A · high conf
A $5\,\text{kg}$ block slides on a rough horizontal surface. A force of $30\,\text{N}$ acts on it at $53^\circ$ above the horizontal. If $\mu=0.2$ and $g=10\,\text{m/s}^2$, what is the block's acceleration?
  1. A $2.56\,\text{m/s}^2$ ✓
  2. B $1.60\,\text{m/s}^2$
  3. C $3.60\,\text{m/s}^2$
  4. D $4.96\,\text{m/s}^2$
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Resolve the pull into horizontal and vertical components.
  2. Use the vertical component to get the normal reaction, then find friction as $\mu N$.
  3. Subtract friction from the horizontal pull and divide by mass.
🔬 Full Solution
Apply Newton's second law along the horizontal direction. Since the applied force is inclined upward, its vertical component reduces the normal reaction and hence changes the frictional force.
Split the $30\,\text{N}$ force into components. Using $\cos53^\circ=\frac{3}{5}$ and $\sin53^\circ=\frac{4}{5}$, the horizontal part is $18\,\text{N}$ and the upward part is $24\,\text{N}$.
$$F_x = 30\cos53^\circ = 18\,\text{N},\quad F_y = 30\sin53^\circ = 24\,\text{N}$$
The block does not accelerate vertically, so the normal reaction balances the reduced weight. With $mg=5\times 10=50\,\text{N}$, the surface reaction becomes $26\,\text{N}$.
$$N + 24 = 50 \Rightarrow N = 26\,\text{N}$$
Kinetic friction is $f=\mu N$. Since the block is sliding, friction opposes the motion with magnitude $0.2\times 26=5.2\,\text{N}$.
$$f = 0.2 \times 26 = 5.2\,\text{N}$$
The net horizontal force is $18-5.2=12.8\,\text{N}$. By $F=ma$, the acceleration is $12.8/5=2.56\,\text{m/s}^2$.
$$a = \frac{12.8}{5} = 2.56\,\text{m/s}^2$$
The acceleration is $2.56\,\text{m/s}^2$, so the correct option is A.
Distractor rationale
  1. A Correct: this uses the horizontal pull $18\,\text{N}$, the reduced normal force $26\,\text{N}$, friction $5.2\,\text{N}$, and then $a=(18-5.2)/5=2.56\,\text{m/s}^2$.
  2. B This comes from taking friction as $0.2\times 50=10\,\text{N}$, which ignores the upward component of the pull; then $a=(18-10)/5=1.60\,\text{m/s}^2$.
  3. C This is obtained by ignoring friction completely, so $a=18/5=3.60\,\text{m/s}^2$.
  4. D This comes from incorrectly using the full $30\,\text{N}$ pull as the net driving force while still subtracting friction: $(30-5.2)/5=4.96\,\text{m/s}^2$.
Q6Medium 🔁 verifier: agrees A · high conf
Block $A$ of mass $2\,\text{kg}$ rests on a rough horizontal table and is tied to a hanging block $B$ of mass $1\,\text{kg}$ by a light, inextensible string passing over a smooth pulley. If the coefficient of kinetic friction between $A$ and the table is $0.2$ and $g=10\,\text{m/s}^2$, what is the tension in the string while the blocks move?
  1. A $8\,\text{N}$ ✓
  2. B $6\,\text{N}$
  3. C $4\,\text{N}$
  4. D $10\,\text{N}$
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Find friction on block $A$ using $f_k=\mu m_A g$.
  2. Use $m_B g-f_k=(m_A+m_B)a$ to get the common acceleration.
  3. Apply $m_B g-T=m_B a$ and solve for $T$.
🔬 Full Solution
Use Newton's second law for the two-block system first so the internal tension cancels out. Then apply Newton's second law to the hanging block to extract the tension.
The friction on the block on the table opposes motion, so its magnitude is found from the normal reaction. Since the table is horizontal, the normal force equals the block's weight.
$$f_k=\mu m_A g=0.2\times2\times10=4\,\text{N}$$
For the combined two-block system, the internal tension does not appear. The external driving force is the hanging weight minus the friction on the table block.
$$F_{\text{net}}=m_B g-f_k=10-4=6\,\text{N}$$
That net force accelerates the total mass of both blocks together. So the common acceleration is obtained by dividing by the total mass.
$$a=\frac{F_{\text{net}}}{m_A+m_B}=\frac{6}{2+1}=2\,\text{m/s}^2$$
Now use Newton's second law on the hanging block alone. Its weight pulls downward and the string tension acts upward, so the difference equals $m_B a$.
$$m_B g-T=m_B a\Rightarrow 10-T=2$$
Solving the last equation gives the string tension.
$$T=8\,\text{N}$$
The tension is $8\,\text{N}$, so the correct option is A.
Distractor rationale
  1. A Correct: using $f_k=4\,\text{N}$ and $a=2\,\text{m/s}^2$, the hanging block gives $10-T=2$, hence $T=8\,\text{N}$.
  2. B This is the net driving force on the system, since $10-4=6\,\text{N}$, but tension is not equal to the external resultant.
  3. C This is only the friction force on block $A$: $0.2\times2\times10=4\,\text{N}$. It is not the tension.
  4. D This is the full weight of the hanging block, $m_B g=1\times10=10\,\text{N}$, which would be the tension only if the block had zero acceleration.

A $4\,\mathrm{kg}$ block $A$ lies on a rough $37^\circ$ incline and is connected by a light inextensible string over a smooth pulley to a hanging $2\,\mathrm{kg}$ block $B$. If $\mu=0.25$, $g=10\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$, and the system remains at rest, what is the magnitude of the frictional force acting on $A$?
  1. A $4\,\mathrm{N}$ ✓
  2. B $8\,\mathrm{N}$
  3. C $20\,\mathrm{N}$
  4. D $24\,\mathrm{N}$
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Write $T = m_B g$ for the hanging block at rest.
  2. Find the downslope pull on $A$ and compare it with $T$.
  3. The leftover force is the friction; check it is below $\mu N$.
🔬 Full Solution
Since the blocks are at rest, each block is in translational equilibrium. Use force balance on the hanging block to get the tension, then balance forces along the incline to get the friction needed; the given $\mu$ only checks that this value is allowed.
Because block $B$ is stationary, the net force on it is zero, so the string tension equals its weight.
$$T = 2 \times 10 = 20\,\mathrm{N}$$
For block $A$, the component of its weight down the plane is $4 \times 10 \times \sin 37^\circ = 24\,\mathrm{N}$, and the normal reaction is $4 \times 10 \times \cos 37^\circ = 32\,\mathrm{N}$.
$$m_A g \sin 37^\circ = 4 \times 10 \times 0.6 = 24\,\mathrm{N}$$
The maximum static friction available is $\mu N = 0.25 \times 32 = 8\,\mathrm{N}$, so the system can indeed stay at rest.
$$\mu N = 0.25 \times 32 = 8\,\mathrm{N}$$
Along the incline, the $20\,\mathrm{N}$ tension balances part of the $24\,\mathrm{N}$ downslope pull, so friction must supply the remaining $4\,\mathrm{N}$ upward.
$$f = 24 - 20 = 4\,\mathrm{N}$$
The frictional force is $4\,\mathrm{N}$, so the correct option is A.
Distractor rationale
  1. A Correct. The hanging block gives $T = 2 \times 10 = 20\,\mathrm{N}$, and the excess downslope pull on $A$ is $24 - 20 = 4\,\mathrm{N}$, so friction is $4\,\mathrm{N}$.
  2. B This is the limiting static friction, $\mu N = 0.25 \times 32 = 8\,\mathrm{N}$, but the actual friction needed here is only $4\,\mathrm{N}$.
  3. C This comes from taking friction equal to the tension, $T = 2 \times 10 = 20\,\mathrm{N}$, which is not the correct balance for this setup.
  4. D This is just the component of $A$'s weight down the plane, $4 \times 10 \times \sin 37^\circ = 24\,\mathrm{N}$, before subtracting the tension from the hanging block.

A $10\,\text{kg}$ block $A$ on a rough horizontal table is connected by a light inextensible string over a smooth pulley to a hanging $7\,\text{kg}$ block $B$. The coefficient of friction between $A$ and the table is $0.2$. A force $F$ acts on $A$ towards the left at an angle of $53^\circ$ above the horizontal. If the system moves with $A$ to the right and $B$ downward with acceleration $1\,\text{m s}^{-2}$, and $\sin 53^\circ = 0.8$, $\cos 53^\circ = 0.6$, what is the magnitude of $F$? Take $g = 10\,\text{m s}^{-2}$.
  1. A $33\,\text{N}$
  2. B $39.29\,\text{N}$
  3. C $55\,\text{N}$
  4. D $75\,\text{N}$ ✓
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Write $7g - T = 7a$ for block $B$ and get $T$.
  2. For block $A$, use $N = 10g - F\sin 53^\circ$ and $f = \mu N$.
  3. Use $T - F\cos 53^\circ - f = 10a$ and solve for $F$.
🔬 Full Solution
Apply Newton's second law separately to the two blocks. Since the applied force is oblique, its vertical component changes the normal reaction, which changes the friction.
For the hanging block $B$, take downward as positive. Its weight pulls it down and the tension pulls it up, so
$$7 \times 10 - T = 7 \times 1$$
This gives the string tension as $T = 63\,\text{N}$.
$$T = 63\,\text{N}$$
For block $A$, the upward component of $F$ reduces the normal reaction. Hence
$$N = 10 \times 10 - F\sin 53^\circ = 100 - 0.8F$$
So the friction is
$$f = 0.2N = 0.2(100 - 0.8F) = 20 - 0.16F$$
Along the horizontal direction for $A$, tension acts to the right, while the horizontal part of $F$ and friction act to the left. Therefore
$$T - F\cos 53^\circ - f = 10 \times 1$$
Substituting $T = 63\,\text{N}$ and the expression for $f$ gives $63 - 0.6F - 0.2(100 - 0.8F) = 10$. This simplifies to $43 - 0.44F = 10$, so $F = 75\,\text{N}$.
$$F = 75\,\text{N}$$
The required force is $75\,\text{N}$, so the correct option is D.
Distractor rationale
  1. A If the applied force is wrongly treated as fully horizontal and friction is fixed at $\mu mg = 20\,\text{N}$, then $63 - F - 20 = 10$, which gives $F = 33\,\text{N}$.
  2. B If one uses the full horizontal pull $F$ but still keeps the reduced normal reaction, then $63 - F - (20 - 0.16F) = 10$, so $0.84F = 33$ and $F \approx 39.29\,\text{N}$.
  3. C If the vertical component of $F$ is ignored, friction stays at $20\,\text{N}$ and the horizontal balance becomes $63 - 0.6F - 20 = 10$, giving $F = 55\,\text{N}$.
  4. D Using $T = 63\,\text{N}$, $N = 100 - 0.8F$, $f = 0.2N$, and $63 - 0.6F - f = 10$ leads to $F = 75\,\text{N}$.
Q9Hard 🔁 verifier: agrees A · high conf
A block $A$ of mass $2\,\mathrm{kg}$ rests on top of block $B$ of mass $1\,\mathrm{kg}$ on a horizontal floor. The coefficient of friction between $A$ and $B$ is $0.5$, and the coefficient of friction between $B$ and the floor is $0.2$. If $g=10\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$ and a horizontal force is applied to $A$ to the right, what is the maximum value of the force for which the two blocks still move together?
  1. A $18\,\mathrm{N}$ ✓
  2. B $10\,\mathrm{N}$
  3. C $12\,\mathrm{N}$
  4. D $16\,\mathrm{N}$
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Find the floor friction on the combined mass: $0.2\times(2+1)\times10=6$ N.
  2. At the slipping limit, the friction between $A$ and $B$ is $0.5\times2\times10=10$ N.
  3. Use $f-6=1a$ and $F-6=3a$ to get $a=4$ and then $F=18$ N.
🔬 Full Solution
Use Newton's second law for the two-block system first, then apply the limiting friction condition at the interface between the blocks. The threshold is reached when the friction needed to drag the lower block equals the maximum available friction between the blocks.
The floor resists the motion of the whole two-block system, so its friction is based on the total normal reaction. Since the total mass on the floor is $2+1=3\,\mathrm{kg}$, the floor friction is $6\,\mathrm{N}$.
$$f_g=0.2\times3\times10=6\,\mathrm{N}$$
While the blocks move together, the only external horizontal force on the pair is $F$ to the right and $6\,\mathrm{N}$ to the left from the floor. So the common acceleration of the two blocks is
$$a=\frac{F-6}{3}$$
The interface between $A$ and $B$ can supply at most limiting static friction. At the verge of slipping, that maximum value is
$$f_{AB,\max}=0.5\times2\times10=10\,\mathrm{N}$$
For block $B$, the rightward friction from $A$ must overcome the $6\,\mathrm{N}$ floor resistance and still produce the acceleration $a$. Hence $f_{AB}-6=1\cdot a$.
$$f_{AB}-6=1\cdot a$$
At the threshold, $f_{AB}=10\,\mathrm{N}$, so $10-6=a$, giving $a=4\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$. Substituting in the system equation gives $F=3a+6=18\,\mathrm{N}$.
$$F=3a+6=18\,\mathrm{N}$$
The maximum force is $18\,\mathrm{N}$, so the correct option is A.
Distractor rationale
  1. A Correct: the floor friction is $6\,\mathrm{N}$, the inter-block limit is $10\,\mathrm{N}$, and Newton's law then gives $a=4\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$ and $F=18\,\mathrm{N}$.
  2. B This comes from treating the top block as if the lower block were fixed, so the only limit is $\mu_{AB}m_Ag=0.5\times2\times10=10\,\mathrm{N}$.
  3. C This uses the lower block's own weight for floor friction, $0.2\times1\times10=2\,\mathrm{N}$, and then adds it to the $10\,\mathrm{N}$ contact limit to get $12\,\mathrm{N}$.
  4. D This adds the correct floor friction and the contact limit directly, $6+10=16\,\mathrm{N}$, but misses that the upper force must also accelerate the lower block.
