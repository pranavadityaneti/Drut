Q1Physics · Motion in a Straight Line · Equations of uniformly accelerated motionEasy
A cart starts from rest and moves along a straight track with uniform acceleration of $2\,\text{m s}^{-2}$ for $5\,\text{s}$. What distance does it cover in this interval?
  1. A$5\,\text{m}$
  2. B$10\,\text{m}$
  3. C$25\,\text{m}$✓
  4. D$50\,\text{m}$
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Take $u=0$ because the cart starts from rest.
  2. Use $s=ut+\frac12 at^2$ and substitute $a=2$ and $t=5$.
  3. Compute $s=\frac12\times 2\times 5^2=25\,\text{m}$ and choose that option.
🔬 Full Solution 3 steps
Use the first equation of motion for uniformly accelerated straight-line motion. Because the cart starts from rest, the displacement formula reduces directly to $s=\frac12 at^2$.
Since the cart starts from rest, its initial velocity is $u=0$. For motion with constant acceleration, the displacement in time $t$ is given by $s=ut+\frac12 at^2$.
$$s=ut+\frac12 at^2$$
Now substitute $u=0$, $a=2\,\text{m s}^{-2}$, and $t=5\,\text{s}$. This gives $s=0+\frac12\times 2\times 5^2$.
$$s=0+\frac12\times 2\times 5^2$$
Since $5^2=25$, we get $s=25\,\text{m}$. So the cart covers $25\,\text{m}$ in $5\,\text{s}$.
$$s=25\,\text{m}$$
Answer: The cart covers $25\,\text{m}$, so the correct option is C.
Q2Chemistry · Some Basic Concepts of Chemistry · Mole concept and limiting reagentMedium
A $10\ \mathrm{g}$ limestone sample contains $80\%$ $\mathrm{CaCO_3}$. It is treated with $200\ \mathrm{mL}$ of $0.5\ \mathrm{M}$ $\mathrm{HCl}$ according to $\mathrm{CaCO_3 + 2HCl \rightarrow CaCl_2 + CO_2 + H_2O}$. What volume of $\mathrm{CO_2}$ at STP is liberated?
  1. A$1.12\ \mathrm{L}$✓
  2. B$1.79\ \mathrm{L}$
  3. C$2.24\ \mathrm{L}$
  4. D$0.56\ \mathrm{L}$
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Convert the $80\%$ purity into moles of $\mathrm{CaCO_3}$ and the acid solution into moles of $\mathrm{HCl}$.
  2. Use the $1:2$ ratio in $\mathrm{CaCO_3 + 2HCl \rightarrow CaCl_2 + CO_2 + H_2O}$ to find the limiting reagent.
  3. Convert the limiting moles of $\mathrm{CO_2}$ to volume at STP using $22.4\ \mathrm{L\,mol^{-1}}$.
🔬 Full Solution 4 steps
Use mole conversion with purity and solution molarity, then apply the balanced equation to identify the limiting reagent. The gas volume comes directly from the stoichiometric moles of $\mathrm{CO_2}$ at STP.
Only $80\%$ of the $10\ \mathrm{g}$ sample is actually $\mathrm{CaCO_3}$, so the reacting mass is $8\ \mathrm{g}$. Using $M_r(\mathrm{CaCO_3})=100$, this gives the amount in moles.
$$n(\mathrm{CaCO_3})=\frac{8}{100}=0.08\ \mathrm{mol}$$
Now find the moles of acid from its molarity: $n=M\times V=0.5\times 0.200=0.10\ \mathrm{mol}$. Since the equation needs $2$ mol of $\mathrm{HCl}$ for $1$ mol of $\mathrm{CaCO_3}$, $0.08\ \mathrm{mol}$ of carbonate would need $0.16\ \mathrm{mol}$ of acid.
$$n(\mathrm{HCl})=0.5\times 0.200=0.10\ \mathrm{mol}$$
Because only $0.10\ \mathrm{mol}$ of $\mathrm{HCl}$ is available, $\mathrm{HCl}$ is the limiting reagent. From the ratio $2\ \mathrm{HCl}:1\ \mathrm{CO_2}$, the carbon dioxide formed is half the moles of limiting acid.
$$n(\mathrm{CO_2})=\frac{0.10}{2}=0.05\ \mathrm{mol}$$
At STP, $1\ \mathrm{mol}$ of gas occupies $22.4\ \mathrm{L}$. Therefore, the volume of $\mathrm{CO_2}$ is $0.05\times 22.4=1.12\ \mathrm{L}$.
$$V=0.05\times 22.4=1.12\ \mathrm{L}$$
Answer: The evolved volume is $1.12\ \mathrm{L}$, so option A is correct.
Q3Mathematics · Quadratic Equations · Nature of roots (discriminant)Medium
For which values of $m$ does the equation $(m-2)x^2 + 2(m-1)x + (m+1)=0$ have two distinct real roots?
  1. A$m<3,\, m\neq 2$✓
  2. B$m\le 3,\, m\neq 2$
  3. C$m>3$
  4. D$m<3$
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Take $a=m-2$, $b=2(m-1)$, and $c=m+1$.
  2. Compute $D=b^2-4ac=4(3-m)$ and require $D>0$.
  3. Also impose $m-2\neq 0$; combine both conditions.
🔬 Full Solution 4 steps
For two distinct real roots, the discriminant must satisfy $D>0$. Since the question is about a quadratic equation, we must also keep the coefficient of $x^2$ nonzero.
For two distinct real roots, the discriminant must satisfy $D>0$. Since the question is about a quadratic equation, we must also keep the coefficient of $x^2$ nonzero.
Here $a=m-2$, $b=2(m-1)$, and $c=m+1$. So $D=b^2-4ac=4(m-1)^2-4(m-2)(m+1)$.
$$D=b^2-4ac=4(m-1)^2-4(m-2)(m+1)$$
Expanding gives $D=4\bigl[m^2-2m+1-(m^2-m-2)\bigr]=4(3-m)$. Therefore $D>0$ means $m<3$.
$$D=4(3-m)$$
Now remove $m=2$, because then $a=0$ and the equation becomes linear, not quadratic. So the required set is $m<3$ with $m\neq2$.
$$m<3,\, m\neq2$$
Answer: The required values are $m<3$ with $m\neq2$, so the correct option is A.
Q4Physics · Laws of Motion · Connected blocks with frictionHard
A $5\ \mathrm{kg}$ block $A$ on a rough horizontal table is connected by a light inextensible string over a smooth pulley at the table edge to a hanging block $B$ of mass $1\ \mathrm{kg}$. A force $P$ is applied to $A$ at $37^\circ$ above the horizontal, directed away from the pulley. If the coefficient of kinetic friction between $A$ and the table is $0.25$, and the system accelerates with $1\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$ with $A$ moving away from the pulley, what is the magnitude of $P$? Take $g=10\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$.
  1. A$30.0\ \mathrm{N}$✓
  2. B$35.6\ \mathrm{N}$
  3. C$29.0\ \mathrm{N}$
  4. D$34.4\ \mathrm{N}$
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Write $T - mg = ma$ for the hanging $1\ \mathrm{kg}$ block.
  2. For block $A$, use $N = 50 - 0.6P$ and $f_k = 0.25N$.
  3. Apply $P\cos37^\circ - T - f_k = 5\times 1$ and solve for $P$.
🔬 Full Solution 4 steps
Use Newton's second law separately for each block. The inclined pull changes the normal reaction on $A$, so the friction must be written as $\mu N$ rather than a fixed value.
Since block $B$ moves upward with acceleration $1\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$, the net upward force on it is $ma$. So for $B$, $T-10=1$, which gives the string tension.
$$T - 10 = 1$$
For block $A$, the upward component of $P$ is $P\sin37^\circ = 0.6P$, so the normal reaction becomes $N = 50 - 0.6P$. Therefore the kinetic friction is $f_k = \mu N = 0.25(50-0.6P) = 12.5 - 0.15P$.
$$N = 50 - 0.6P,\quad f_k = 0.25(50 - 0.6P)$$
Along the horizontal direction for $A$, the leftward driving force is $P\cos37^\circ = 0.8P$, while tension and friction act to the right. Since $A$ accelerates left with $1\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$,
$$0.8P - T - f_k = 5$$
Substituting $T=11$ and $f_k = 12.5 - 0.15P$, we get $0.8P - 11 - (12.5 - 0.15P) = 5$. This simplifies to $0.95P = 28.5$, so $P = 30\ \mathrm{N}$.
$$0.95P = 28.5\quad \Rightarrow\quad P = 30\ \mathrm{N}$$
Answer: $P = 30\ \mathrm{N}$, so the correct option is A.

  1. Divide the $2SO_2(g)+O_2(g)\rightarrow 2SO_3(g)$ equation by $2$.

  3. Add the enthalpies and choose the option with the final value.

$$S(s)+H_2(g)+2O_2(g)\rightarrow H_2SO_4(l)$$
By Hess's law, the total enthalpy change is the sum of the adjusted values. Substituting the numbers gives $-296.8-99.1-130.4-285.8$.
$$\Delta H=-296.8-99.1-130.4-285.8$$
Evaluating the sum gives $-812.1\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$. So the reaction is exothermic and the correct choice is option B.
$$\Delta H=-812.1\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$$
Answer: $\Delta H=-812.1\ \mathrm{kJ\,mol^{-1}}$, option B.
Q6Chemistry · Structure of Atom · Quantum numbersEasy
Which of the following quantum-number sets cannot describe a single electron?
  1. A$n=4, l=0, m_l=0, m_s=+\frac{1}{2}$
  2. B$n=3, l=2, m_l=-1, m_s=-\frac{1}{2}$
  3. C$n=2, l=1, m_l=1, m_s=+\frac{1}{2}$
  4. D$n=3, l=3, m_l=0, m_s=+\frac{1}{2}$✓
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Check whether $l$ lies from $0$ to $n-1$.
  2. If that passes, check whether $m_l$ lies from $-l$ to $+l$ and whether $m_s$ is $\pm \frac{1}{2}$.
  3. Reject the option that breaks any rule; here $n=3$ with $l=3$ fails immediately.
🔬 Full Solution 5 steps
Quantum numbers are accepted only when they satisfy fixed ranges. The fastest check is to test $l$ against $n$, then $m_l$, and finally $m_s$.
The allowed ranges are $0 \le l \le n-1$, $-l \le m_l \le l$, and $m_s=\pm \frac{1}{2}$. So the first thing to test is whether each set respects these limits.
$$0 \le l \le n-1,\ -l \le m_l \le l,\ m_s=\pm \frac{1}{2}$$
Option A has $n=4$, so $l=0$ is permitted because $l$ may be $0,1,2,3$. With $l=0$, the only possible value is $m_l=0$, and $m_s=+\frac{1}{2}$ is valid.
Option B has $n=3$ and $l=2$, which is allowed because $l$ can be $0,1,2$. Also, $m_l=-1$ lies between $-2$ and $2$, and $m_s=-\frac{1}{2}$ is valid.
Option C has $n=2$ and $l=1$, which is allowed because $l$ can be $0$ or $1$. Here $m_l=1$ lies in the range $-1$ to $1$, and $m_s=+\frac{1}{2}$ is also valid.
Option D fails immediately because for $n=3$, the largest permitted value of $l$ is $n-1=2$. Since the option gives $l=3$, it violates the principal-azimuthal rule and cannot represent an electron.
$$n-1=2$$
Answer: Option D is correct.
Q7Physics · Motion in a Plane · Projectile motion — range and time of flightMedium
A projectile is launched from level ground and returns to the same level after $4$ s. If its horizontal range is $96$ m and $g=10\,\text{m/s}^2$, what is the speed of projection?
  1. A$4\sqrt{61}\,\text{m/s}$✓
  2. B$24\sqrt{2}\,\text{m/s}$
  3. C$20\sqrt{2}\,\text{m/s}$
  4. D$44\,\text{m/s}$
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Find the horizontal component from $u\cos\theta=R/T=96/4=24$.
  2. Find the vertical component from $u\sin\theta=gT/2=10\times 4/2=20$.
  3. Combine them: $u=\sqrt{24^2+20^2}=4\sqrt{61}$, and choose the matching option.
🔬 Full Solution 4 steps
Use the two standard projectile relations for motion on the same level: $R=u\cos\theta\,T$ and $T=\frac{2u\sin\theta}{g}$. Once the horizontal and vertical components are found, the launch speed follows from combining perpendicular components.
For a projectile that lands back at the launch level, the horizontal speed remains constant, so the range is the horizontal component multiplied by the total time of flight.
$$u\cos\theta = \frac{R}{T}$$
Substituting the given values, the horizontal component becomes $u\cos\theta = 96/4 = 24\,\text{m/s}$.
$$u\cos\theta = \frac{96}{4} = 24$$
The time-of-flight formula gives the vertical component. Since $T=\frac{2u\sin\theta}{g}$, we get $u\sin\theta=\frac{gT}{2}=\frac{10\times 4}{2}=20\,\text{m/s}$.
$$u\sin\theta = \frac{gT}{2} = \frac{10\times 4}{2} = 20$$
Now combine the perpendicular components to get the launch speed:
$$u = \sqrt{(u\cos\theta)^2 + (u\sin\theta)^2} = \sqrt{24^2+20^2} = \sqrt{976} = 4\sqrt{61}$$
Answer: The speed of projection is $4\sqrt{61}\,\text{m/s}$, so the correct option is A.
Q8Mathematics · Trigonometry · Trigonometric identities and equationsMedium
How many distinct values of $x \in [0,2\pi)$ satisfy $2\sin^2 x + 3\sin x \cos x - \cos^2 x = 0$?
  1. A2
  2. B4✓
  3. C6
  4. D8
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Check the special case $\cos x=0$ first; it will not satisfy the equation.
  2. For $\cos x \ne 0$, divide by $\cos^2 x$ and put $t=\tan x$ to get a quadratic in $t$.
  3. Solve the quadratic; each real finite tangent value gives two angles in $[0,2\pi)$, so count them.
🔬 Full Solution 4 steps
The equation is homogeneous in $\sin x$ and $\cos x$, so the fastest route is to convert it into a quadratic in $\tan x$. After that, the answer comes from counting how many angles each tangent value produces in one full cycle.
First, handle the case $\cos x=0$ separately, because the tangent substitution would not apply there. If $\cos x=0$, then $\sin x=\pm1$, and the left side becomes $2\sin^2 x=2$, so this case gives no solution.
$$2\sin^2 x = 2$$
Now take $\cos x \ne 0$ and divide the equation by $\cos^2 x$. Writing $t=\tan x$, we get a quadratic equation in $t$.
$$2t^2 + 3t - 1 = 0$$
Solving gives $t=\frac{-3\pm\sqrt{17}}{4}$. Both roots are real and finite, so both are admissible tangent values.
$$t=\frac{-3\pm\sqrt{17}}{4}$$
A finite tangent value produces exactly two angles in $[0,2\pi)$, namely one in each half of the period. Therefore the two roots contribute $2+2=4$ solutions in total.
$$2\times 2 = 4$$
Answer: There are $4$ solutions, so the correct option is B.
Q9Physics · Oscillations · Energy in simple harmonic motionHard
A particle in SHM has amplitude $A$. At two positions on the same side of the mean position, the displacements are $x_1$ and $x_2$ with $x_1 = 2x_2$. If the kinetic energies at those positions are in the ratio $3:8$, then $x_1/A$ is:
  1. A$\sqrt{\frac{20}{29}}$✓
  2. B$\sqrt{\frac{5}{29}}$
  3. C$\sqrt{\frac{20}{31}}$
  4. D$\sqrt{\frac{10}{13}}$
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Use $K/E = 1 - x^2/A^2$ for each position.
  2. Set $y = x_2^2/A^2$ and replace $x_1^2/A^2$ by $4y$.
  3. Solve the ratio, then take the square root to get $x_1/A$.
🔬 Full Solution 4 steps
The governing idea is that in SHM, energy depends only on displacement: $U \propto x^2$ and $K = E - U$. With $x_1 = 2x_2$, one variable is enough to extract $x_1/A$ from the energy ratio.
For a mass in SHM, the total energy is $E = \frac{1}{2}kA^2$, while at displacement $x$ the potential energy is $U = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$. Hence the kinetic energy at that point is $K = E - U = E\left(1 - \frac{x^2}{A^2}\right)$.
$$K = E\left(1 - \frac{x^2}{A^2}\right)$$
Let $y = x_2^2/A^2$. Since $x_1 = 2x_2$, we get $x_1^2/A^2 = 4y$. The given ratio $K_1:K_2 = 3:8$ becomes a single equation in $y$.
$$\frac{1-4y}{1-y} = \frac{3}{8}$$
Cross-multiplying gives $8(1 - 4y) = 3(1 - y)$, so $8 - 32y = 3 - 3y$. Rearranging, $5 = 29y$, hence $y = 5/29$.
$$8 - 32y = 3 - 3y$$
Therefore $x_1^2/A^2 = 4y = 20/29$, and taking the positive root for a displacement magnitude gives $x_1/A = \sqrt{20/29}$. So the correct choice is option A.
$$\frac{x_1}{A} = \sqrt{\frac{20}{29}}$$
Answer: Option A: $\sqrt{\frac{20}{29}}$.
Q10Mathematics · Sequences and Series · Sum to n terms (AP/GP)Hard
An AP has first term $5$ and common difference $10$. A GP has first term $5$ and common ratio $2$. For the same positive integer $n$, the sum of the first $n$ terms of the GP exceeds the sum of the first $n$ terms of the AP by $5(2n+15)$. Then $n$ is:
  1. A$4$
  2. B$5$
  3. C$6$✓
  4. D$7$
⚡ Quick Method
  1. Use $S_n^{AP}=\frac n2[2a+(n-1)d]$ to get $S_n^{AP}=5n^2$.
  2. Use $S_n^{GP}=\frac{a(r^n-1)}{r-1}$ to get $S_n^{GP}=5(2^n-1)$.
  3. Set $S_n^{GP}-S_n^{AP}=5(2n+15)$ and simplify to $2^n=n^2+2n+16$; then test the options.
🔬 Full Solution 4 steps
Use the standard sum formulas for an AP and a GP. Writing both sums in terms of $n$ turns the given excess condition into one equation in $n$.
For the AP, substitute $a=5$ and $d=10$ into the sum formula. This gives a simple quadratic expression for the first $n$ terms.
$$S_n^{AP}=\frac n2\,[2\cdot 5+(n-1)\cdot 10]=5n^2$$
For the GP, substitute $a=5$ and $r=2$ into the GP sum formula. This gives the sum in exponential form.
$$S_n^{GP}=\frac{5(2^n-1)}{2-1}=5(2^n-1)$$
The GP sum exceeds the AP sum by $5(2n+15)$, so write the difference and simplify. After dividing by $5$, the condition becomes a single equation in $n$.
$$5(2^n-1)-5n^2=5(2n+15)\Rightarrow 2^n=n^2+2n+16$$
Now check the integer choices. Only $n=6$ satisfies the equation because $2^6=64$ and $6^2+2\cdot 6+16=64$.
$$2^6=6^2+2\cdot 6+16$$
Answer: Therefore, $n=6$, so the correct option is C.
