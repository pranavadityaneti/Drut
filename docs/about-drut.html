I · What Drut is

A practice platform, not a tutor.

A distinction worth making A tutor is someone you go to for answers. A practice platform is something you return to so the answer becomes automatic. India's exam aspirants do not lose to understanding; they lose to time, to forgetting, and to the fifteenth question they have never seen. Drut is built for that fifteenth question.

Drut is a prep tech platform. We serve students preparing for India's competitive entrance examinations — state engineering and medical exams, national exams like JEE Main, JEE Advanced, NEET, BITSAT and CUET, and in time, exams as broad as CAT. The platform is exam-agnostic at its core; the content is exam-specific.

For our MVP we have narrowed deliberately to a single audience: students writing the Andhra Pradesh and Telangana EAPCET — about six hundred and sixty thousand aspirants in 2024 alone. This is the audience we serve at launch. Every other exam on the platform's roadmap inherits the same architecture, the same textbook-grounded practice loop, and the same forgetting-protection signals.

We are not an AI tutor. We do not pretend to teach. What we do is open a particular student's textbook to a particular page, generate as many questions as that student needs to internalize the concept on it, watch which shortcuts they actually use, and refuse to let them move on until the pattern sticks.

II · Exams on the platform

One platform. Built to carry every exam India studies for.

The architecture — textbook ingestion, chunked retrieval, pattern-aware question generation, debt tracking, sprint pacing — is the same regardless of the exam. The cost of adding an exam is the cost of ingesting its prescribed textbooks. The MVP is narrow on purpose; the platform is not.

III · What is built today

A working pipeline, in production.

Stack React on Vercel. Supabase Postgres with pgvector. Gemini 2.5 Pro for table-of-contents extraction. Gemini 2.5 Flash for question generation. gemini-embedding-001 for the 768-dimensional vector space. Custom retrieval, custom chunking, custom intervention engine.

Why this matters The system runs end-to-end. A student request becomes an embedding becomes a retrieval becomes a textbook-anchored question in under fifteen seconds. Or, if pre-warmed in the cache, instantly.

The ingestion pipeline accepts a PDF, extracts text per-page in the browser, sends only the text array to the server. The server strips repeating headers and footers by frequency analysis, chunks the cleaned text with page numbers preserved, embeds each chunk into a 768-dimensional vector, and stores them with chapter and subject metadata.

Retrieval works as follows. A student asks for practice on Functions in NCERT Class 11 Mathematics. The system embeds the query string. Vector similarity against the 7,204 chunks returns the top five. Those chunks are stitched into a context block, sent to Gemini Flash with a prompt that demands a multiple-choice question whose every numerical value, every formula, every concept must trace to the provided context.

The generated question is stamped v3-verified-rag — meaning anchored to a textbook page — or v3-unverified-ai when retrieval found nothing. Students never see the second category in production.

A pre-warming pass has already run across every chapter on the platform: thirty grounded questions per chapter, ahead of time. The cache is full. Students hit it instantly, then can keep going. Finite, then infinite.

Other platforms are curriculum-led — they teach the concept and move on. Drut is exam-led — the curriculum is the second Monday in May, the timer is sixty seconds, and the only thing that matters is whether the student can recognise the pattern under pressure.
IV · Practice

Unlimited questions. Per chapter. Per session.

Practice is the core of the platform. The student picks an exam, a subject, one or several chapters, a difficulty, a question count — and Drut keeps generating fresh, textbook-grounded questions for as long as the student wants to keep going. The bottleneck is the syllabus, not the question bank.

  1. i
    Finite to infinite Pre-generated cache for instant first questions; live retrieval-and-generation for the rest. A student can practice the same chapter on Monday and Friday and never see the same question twice. Every question is anchored to a page of their actual textbook.
  2. ii
    Multi-chapter selection Checkboxes against the actual chapters extracted from the student's syllabus. Not a fixed taxonomy. Chapter 5: Laws of Motion from NCERT, not Newtonian Mechanics.
  3. iii
    Difficulty bands · Easy, Medium, Hard, Mixed Mixed serves the student a realistic distribution. Hard surfaces previous-year-paper style discriminators. Easy lets a beginner build streaks before they take on the difficult ones.
  4. iv
    The Fastest Safe Method (FSM) Competitive exams are time-bound. Knowing how to solve a problem is not the same as knowing how to solve it in sixty seconds. When the student gets a question wrong — or right, but slowly — a small intervention appears. It names the optimal shortcut (the inclusion-exclusion trick, the partial-fraction shortcut, the vector-projection identity) and shows the abbreviated path. That named shortcut is what the system tracks.
  5. v
    Full step-by-step, on demand Alongside the FSM, the student can open the complete worked solution — every step, every justification — so the concept is reinforced without compromising the timing discipline. The shortcut for the next question; the full method for the long-term memory.
  6. vi
    Skip-with-debt If a student closes the intervention without acknowledging the optimal path, the system records mastery debt against that FSM tag. The debt resurfaces in subsequent sessions, prompts after prompts, until the student practices it. We do not let a student avoid the patterns they pretended to understand.
  7. vii
    Exam-realistic timing EAPCET allots sixty seconds per question. JEE Main allots one hundred and twenty. CAT runs on a different rhythm. Drut times sessions to the actual exam pattern of the student's target exam — not "average reading time" or some abstract benchmark.
  8. viii
    Pause and resume Practice sessions persist across devices and disconnections. A student can step away mid-session and continue exactly where they left off.
  9. ix
    Trusted-source filter, always on Students only see questions whose verification status is one of: previous-year paper, textbook-RAG-grounded, or curator-verified. Unverified AI never reaches the practice queue.
V · Sprint

A timer that runs, even between questions.

Why Sprint exists Knowing a topic and performing on it inside three hours, with the clock visible, are two different skills. The real exams are stressful precisely because the timer does not stop when the student hesitates. Sprint trains for that pressure.

Cadence Recommended: one Sprint per chapter per week. Build up to full-syllabus Sprints in the month before the exam.

Sprint is the high-pressure counterpart to Practice. The clock runs in the background even when the student is staring at a question. There is no pause button. There is no intervention modal mid-session. Sprint waits until the run is over before offering review.

Sprint timings mirror the actual exam: sixty seconds per question for EAPCET, one hundred and twenty for JEE Main, the appropriate ratio for whichever exam the student is preparing for. Some Sprints are topical — one chapter, fifteen questions, fifteen minutes. Some are full-syllabus — 160 questions, three hours, the actual exam shape.

What makes Drut Sprint unlike a mock test from a coaching center is the post-session report. The system reconstructs every question against the student's full history. Was this question hit a debt tag? Was the wrong answer the same wrong answer they gave last month? Did they speed up under pressure, or did they panic and skim? The report is not "you scored 87 out of 160." It is "you scored 87 out of 160 and lost twenty-two marks on patterns we already flagged for revision; here is the chapter to drill before next Sunday."

VI · What we measure

Per student, per pattern, per chapter, per attempt.

For every question a student attempts, Drut records the atoms: correct or wrong, time taken, option chosen, intervention triggered, FSM acknowledged, chapter and subject of origin. From those atoms the platform derives a layered set of signals. Some are live today. Some are designed for what we measure next.

  • Streakconsecutive days practiced
    The simplest loyalty signal. Visible to the student and, when enabled, to a parent dashboard. The compounding habit a competitive-exam aspirant needs in the months before their paper.
  • Topic Focuslive accuracy on the current subtopic
    Live percentage on the chapter the student is currently practicing. Updates after every question. The student sees, in real time, whether they are getting better.
  • Pattern Masterynamed FSM, acknowledged and reproduced
    For each named technique — the inclusion-exclusion trick, the partial-fraction shortcut, the vector-projection identity — the system tracks how many times the student has produced it correctly under timed conditions. Patterns progress from introduced to verified to mastered.
  • Speedaverage seconds per correct answer
    Trended over a fourteen-day window. Visible alongside accuracy. Slow and right is acceptable for a learner. Fast and wrong is dangerous. Fast and right is the exam-ready state.
  • Pacedistribution, not just average
    A histogram of time-per-question. Reveals the student who averages sixty seconds but actually answers half in twenty and half in ninety — a fragile profile under exam pressure. Average speed lies; the distribution does not.
  • Focus Areasbottom-three weakest subtopics
    Ranked by accuracy across the student's last ninety attempts. The home screen always carries three actionable suggestions: here is what to drill next, and why.
  • Learning Velocityvolume and accuracy, last 30 days
    A simple chart. Volume and accuracy on one axis, time on the other. Parents see this most. It is the answer to "is my child working, and is it working."
  • Debt Collectorunpaid FSM tags resurfacing
    A ranked queue of patterns where the student previously skipped past an intervention. The system holds the debt. Until the student demonstrates the shortcut under timed conditions, the chapter keeps reappearing — softly at first, then insistently.

What we are adding next.

The signals below are designed for the next analytics release. Each is intended to surface what the basic accuracy chart hides: did the student actually remember this, or did they get lucky last week?

  • Retention Pulseforgetting curve, per pattern
    For each FSM the student "mastered" weeks ago, predict the probability they still remember it today. Surface the patterns at risk before the student notices the decay. The spaced-repetition queue follows from this signal.
  • Cold-start Recallperformance on a topic not seen for 7+ days
    The real measure of mastery is not how a student does on a topic they practiced yesterday, but how they do on a topic they have not touched for a week. Cold-start Recall is that measurement — and it is the one most likely to be honest.
  • Mistake Taxonomyconceptual, careless, time-pressure
    Not every wrong answer is the same wrong answer. The system distinguishes a wrong answer caused by misunderstanding (conceptual), by arithmetic slip (careless), and by running out of clock (time). The remedy for each is different. The student sees which one they keep doing.
  • Exam Readiness Scorecomposite estimate of marks if the exam were today
    A running estimate of the student's mark on a full-paper attempt of their target exam, derived from accuracy, speed, FSM coverage, and Retention Pulse against the syllabus weightage. Not a leaderboard. A diagnostic.
  • Time-to-Masteryattempts till consistent correct
    For each pattern, how many practice attempts the student needed to reach reliable mastery. Becomes a per-student learning-rate profile over time — useful to the platform's adaptive engine, and to the parent who wants to know whether their child is improving in the way that matters.
  • Negative-Marking Riskguess-rate against the exam's penalty
    Most Indian competitive exams penalize wrong answers. The system tracks the gap between the student's accuracy on the questions they attempted and what their accuracy would have been if they had skipped the bottom-confidence quartile. Tells the student when to leave a question blank.
  • Concept Graph Coverageprerequisite chain mastery
    A subtopic in calculus depends on derivatives; derivatives depend on functions; functions depend on sets. When a student struggles with the latter, the system traces back the dependency chain and surfaces the actual missing prerequisite, not the symptom.
  • Weekly Parent Briefa written summary, in plain language
    A short written report sent every week to a parent's email or WhatsApp. Practice volume, accuracy direction, two patterns mastered, one chapter to revise this Sunday. Not a dashboard. A letter.
VII · Why these signals help a child

Three things separate Drut's analytics from a scoreboard.

For parents A weekly summary in plain language: "your daughter practiced 240 questions this week, accuracy improved on 6 subtopics, speed declined on 1, here is the chapter to revise before Sunday." No coaching-center receipt. No vague reassurance. The actual chapter, the actual page.

One. The granularity is per-student, per-pattern, per-chapter. Not "Math: 47 percent." That number is useless. Drut tells the student: You solve Sets union problems at 71 percent. But the inclusion-exclusion shortcut you got wrong nine out of twelve times — let us drill that one technique for three minutes.

Two. Every datum is tied to a textbook page. When the system says "your accuracy on Application of Derivatives is sliding," it can open NCERT Class 12 Mathematics Part I, page 197, the exact paragraph that defines the technique the student forgot. The student sees the source, not a paraphrase of the source.

Three. Most edtech surfaces what you got right. Drut surfaces what you skipped past, what you almost knew, what you need to revisit. The Debt Collector and Retention Pulse are, in some sense, the entire product. They are the parts that respect the student's intelligence — that say, you and I both know you guessed that one; let us address it.

Long-horizon

Spaced repetition engine · Adaptive difficulty · First-week diagnostic

A real spaced-repetition engine built around the FSM-debt and Retention Pulse signals. Per-student adaptive difficulty that holds the student in the productive-struggle zone. A first-week diagnostic that places a student precisely on the syllabus and recommends a six-month plan.

IX · How Drut is different

Other platforms deliver content. Drut delivers practice.

The honest answer is that Drut occupies a position no major Indian player has chosen to defend: a practice platform first, content second, brand last. Every alternative below makes a different choice — and we think their choices leave room.
